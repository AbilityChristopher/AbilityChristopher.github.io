Breaking the Silence: Menstrual Hygiene Management in Schools

📅 2026 ⏱ 6 min read ✍️ Ability Christopher

There is a conversation happening in Nigerian classrooms that most people would rather avoid. It involves blood, shame, missed school days, and a silence so heavy it has become its own kind of harm. I am talking about menstrual health — and the urgent need to bring honest, stigma-free education about it into every school in this country.

This is not a comfortable topic. But discomfort is not a reason to stay silent. And the evidence is clear: when we stay silent, girls pay the price.

What I Witnessed in the Field

As part of my NYSC service with Ser-Av Foundation, I participated in Menstrual Hygiene Management (MHM) sensitisation sessions across four schools in two communities in Benue State — LGEA Primary School Kanshio, UBE Kanshio, NKST Primary School Wadata, and UBE Wadata.

What I saw confirmed what the research has long shown: many girls are navigating their menstrual health entirely alone, with no accurate information, inadequate facilities, and surrounded by myths and shame that make everything harder. Girls missing school during their period because they have no sanitary products and fear embarrassment. Teachers refusing to discuss the topic because it is considered inappropriate. Boys mocking girls who show any sign of discomfort. The silence making everything worse.

When a girl cannot manage her period with dignity, she cannot fully participate in her own education. It is that simple.

Why This Is an Education Issue

Menstrual health is not just a health issue. It is a girls education issue. Studies consistently show that girls who lack access to proper menstrual hygiene management miss several school days per month during their period. Over an academic year, that adds up to weeks of lost learning — a gap that compounds over time and contributes to girls falling behind or dropping out entirely.

And yet, in most Nigerian schools, there is no formal provision for menstrual health education. No clean private toilets. No access to sanitary products. No trained teacher to handle the conversation. Just silence — and the damage that silence does.

The Role of Sensitisation

The MHM sessions I facilitated were not just about handing out information. They were about creating a safe space where students — boys and girls — could ask questions, challenge myths, and begin to see menstruation as a normal biological process rather than something shameful or dirty.

The response was telling. Girls who had never spoken about their experiences began to share. Boys who had mocked classmates began to understand. Teachers who had avoided the topic began to see it differently. A single conversation, handled with care and honesty, can shift years of silence.

What Needs to Happen

Clean facilities. Schools need private, functional toilets that girls can use safely and with dignity during their period. This is not optional — it is a basic requirement for girls to attend school without fear.

Access to sanitary products. Sanitary pads should not be a luxury. Schools, communities, and government programmes need to ensure that no girl misses school because she cannot afford basic hygiene products.

Curriculum inclusion. Menstrual health education needs to be part of the formal school curriculum — taught by trained teachers, in age-appropriate ways, to both boys and girls.

Community buy-in. Parents, community leaders, and religious figures all have a role to play in normalising this conversation. Shame is sustained by community silence. It is ended the same way.

A Call to Action

If you are an educator, a parent, a community leader, or a young person reading this — you have a role to play. Talk about this. Normalise this. Advocate for the girls in your community who are suffering in silence.

The silence is not protecting anyone. It is only making the problem worse. Breaking it is where the healing begins.
