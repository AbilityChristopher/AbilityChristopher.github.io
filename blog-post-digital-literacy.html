Digital Literacy: A Necessity, Not a Luxury

📅 2026 ⏱ 5 min read ✍️ Ability Christopher

We live in a world that is moving online at a pace many Nigerians have not been prepared for. From banking to job applications, from healthcare to education — the digital space is no longer a nice addition to daily life. It is the main stage. And if you are not on it, you are being left behind.

This is not an exaggeration. It is the reality I witnessed firsthand during my work as a certified facilitator under the NITDA DL4ALL initiative — and it is a reality that demands an urgent, honest response.

The Gap Is Real

During my work facilitating digital literacy sessions across Benue State, I sat with hundreds of Nigerians in the informal sector — traders, artisans, small business owners, women in community groups — who had never sent an email, never used mobile banking, and had no idea how to protect themselves from online scams.

These are not unintelligent people. They are hardworking, resourceful individuals who simply were never taught the language of the digital world. That is the gap. Not intelligence. Not effort. Access to knowledge. And access to knowledge is something we can do something about.

Digital exclusion is the new illiteracy. And just like illiteracy, it is not the fault of the individual — it is a failure of the systems around them.

What Digital Literacy Actually Means

Many people hear "digital literacy" and think it means knowing how to code or operate complex software. It does not. At its most foundational level, digital literacy means being able to navigate a smartphone confidently, send and receive emails, use mobile banking safely, identify scams and protect personal information, and use the internet to find information, access services, and communicate.

These are not advanced skills. But without them, a person cannot apply for most formal jobs, cannot access government services online, cannot grow a small business in the digital economy, and is vulnerable to the financial fraud that is rampant in our online spaces. The cost of digital exclusion is not abstract — it is measured in lost income, missed opportunities, and avoidable harm.

Why It Matters More Than Ever

Nigeria has over 100 million internet users — but digital penetration still leaves out millions, particularly in rural areas and among older populations. Meanwhile, the government, banks, and employers are moving more and more processes online. If we do not deliberately bring people along, we create a two-tier society: those who can participate in the digital economy, and those permanently locked out of it.

The NITDA DL4ALL initiative is one of the most important federal programmes addressing this gap — and being part of it as a facilitator gave me a front-row seat to both the scale of the problem and the power of the solution. When someone who has never touched a smartphone successfully sends their first message or completes their first mobile transfer, something shifts in them. Confidence. Possibility. Access.

What We Can Do

The solution is not complicated. It requires deliberate, community-based digital literacy programmes that meet people where they are — in markets, community halls, churches, mosques, and schools. It requires facilitators who speak the local language, understand the context, and can make digital tools feel relevant rather than foreign.

It also requires young educated Nigerians — especially those of us with the privilege of formal education — to take responsibility for bringing knowledge back to our communities. Digital literacy is not just a government project. It is a community responsibility.

A Final Word

If you can read this article on a screen, you already have an advantage that millions of your fellow Nigerians do not. That advantage comes with a responsibility. Use your digital skills. Share what you know. Advocate for inclusion. And if you are in a position to teach — teach.

The digital world is not going to slow down for anyone. But together, we can make sure no one is left behind.
